### Beat Captions

### Voice Script

Welcome to English Grammar. Today we are building sentence power, one clear idea at a time. First, think of a sentence as a little machine. A machine needs parts that work together. A sentence needs a subject, a predicate, and a complete thought. The subject is the who or what. In the sentence, The curious dog chased the red ball, the subject is the curious dog. That is who the sentence is about. The predicate tells what happened. Chased the red ball is the predicate. It gives the action and finishes the idea. Now try a smaller one: Maya reads the comic. Maya is the subject. Reads the comic is the predicate. If I only say, reads the comic, your brain asks, who reads the comic? If I only say Maya, your brain asks, what about Maya? A strong sentence answers both questions. Here is your trick: ask, who or what is this about? Then ask, what happened or what is true? When both answers are there, you probably have a complete sentence.

### Voice Script

Now we meet nouns more carefully. A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea. Teacher is a person. Garden is a place. Bicycle is a thing. Courage is an idea. A proper noun names one special person or place, like Maya or London, so it begins with a capital letter. A common noun names a general thing, like girl, city, or book. Next comes number. Singular means one. Plural means more than one. One fox becomes two foxes. One baby becomes two babies. One leaf becomes two leaves. English has patterns, but it also has exceptions, so good readers watch the ending. Gender can tell whether a noun is masculine, feminine, common, or neuter. Boy is masculine. Girl is feminine. Teacher can be common because it could refer to anyone. Desk is neuter because it is not male or female. Finally, case tells the noun's job. In Maya read the book, Maya is doing the action. In Omar helped Maya, Maya receives the action. In Maya's book, the apostrophe shows possession. Quick check: in The girls carried Sam's boxes, girls is plural and doing the action, and Sam's shows possession.

## 3. Verbs, Tense And Verb Jobs

- Duration: 1:40
- Teaching point: Verbs show action or being. Tense tells when it happens, and verb jobs show how the action works.
- Audio: assets/audio/03-verbs-tense.mp3

### Beat Captions

- 0:00: Verbs are doing or being words.
- 0:22: Tense tells time: past, present, or future.
- 0:48: Verbs can be transitive, intransitive, linking, auxiliary, or modal.
- 1:14: Ask whether the verb needs an object, links an idea, or helps another verb.

### Voice Script

Verbs are the engine words. They show action or being. Jump, think, build, laugh, is, were, and has are all verbs. Verbs also carry time. That time is called tense. Past tense means it happened before. Present tense means it is happening now, or it is generally true. Future tense means it will happen later. Yesterday I walked. Today I walk. Tomorrow I will walk. Now add the verb jobs. A transitive verb passes action to an object: Maya kicked the ball. Kicked needs the object ball. An intransitive verb does not need an object: The baby laughed. A linking verb connects the subject to a description: The soup is hot. Is links soup and hot. An auxiliary verb helps the main verb: She has finished. Has helps finished. A modal verb shows possibility, ability, or duty: can, could, should, must, and might. Quick check: in Omar should read the poem, should is a modal helper and read is the main verb. Strong writers choose the right tense and notice the job the verb is doing.

### Voice Script

Adjectives and adverbs are detail tools. They help the reader picture the idea more clearly. An adjective describes a noun. If I say kite, you can picture a kite. If I say bright blue kite, the picture becomes stronger. Bright and blue are adjectives because they describe the noun kite. Adjectives often answer what kind, which one, or how many. Now adverbs usually describe verbs. If I say the turtle moved, you know the action. If I say the turtle moved slowly, you know how it moved. Slowly is an adverb. Adverbs can answer how, when, where, or how much. Here is a sentence: The brave girl spoke clearly. Brave describes the noun girl, so brave is an adjective. Clearly describes the verb spoke, so clearly is an adverb. One warning: do not add detail just to decorate. Add detail when it helps meaning. The tiny silver key opened the old gate is useful detail. The very extremely amazingly shiny tiny silver key can become too heavy. Good writers choose details like careful artists.

### Voice Script

Prepositions are small words with a big job. They show relationships. They can show position, direction, time, or connection. Listen to these: on, under, beside, between, after, before, during, into, across, and near. If I say the book is on the desk, you know where it is. If I say the book is under the desk, the picture changes completely. A preposition often begins a prepositional phrase. Under the table is a phrase. After lunch is a phrase. Near the tall tree is a phrase. These phrases add helpful detail. But be careful: a prepositional phrase is usually not the main subject or the main verb. In the sentence, The cat slept under the chair, the subject is cat and the verb is slept. Under the chair tells where the cat slept. Here is your quick test. If a word tells where, when, or how two things are related, it may be a preposition. Prepositions are like tiny signposts. They tell the reader where to look.

## 6. Conjunctions

- Duration: 1:40
- Teaching point: Conjunctions connect words, phrases, and clauses.
- Audio: assets/audio/06-conjunctions.mp3

### Beat Captions

- 0:00: Conjunctions are joining words.
- 0:22: And adds. But contrasts. Or offers a choice. So gives a result.
- 0:52: A comma often comes before a conjunction joining two full sentences.
- 1:18: Choose the connector that matches the relationship.

### Voice Script

Conjunctions are joining words. They connect words, phrases, or complete ideas. A useful set to remember is for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so. Many students remember them as fanboys. And adds something. I packed a pencil and a ruler. But shows contrast. I wanted to play, but it rained. Or gives a choice. We can read or draw. So shows a result. I was hungry, so I made toast. The clever part is choosing the connector that matches the relationship between ideas. If two ideas go together, and may work. If one idea pushes against the other, but may work. If one idea causes the next, so may work. When you join two complete sentences with a conjunction, you often put a comma before the conjunction. I finished my homework, so I played outside. Both sides could stand as sentences. Quick check: I wore a coat, because it was cold. Because explains the reason. Conjunctions are bridges. Good bridges make the sentence easy to cross.

### Voice Script

Punctuation and capitals are reading signals. They tell the reader where ideas start, where they stop, and how they should sound. Start every sentence with a capital letter. Capitalize names, places, days, months, and important titles. We write Monday with a capital M. We write Leo with a capital L. End marks matter too. A period ends a telling sentence. They separate items in a list: I packed a pen, a snack, and a hat. They also often appear before a conjunction that joins two complete sentences. I wanted to keep reading, but the bell rang. Now watch the repair: on monday leo asked where is my pencil. It should become: On Monday, Leo asked, Where is my pencil? Better punctuation makes the sentence easier to hear inside your head. It is not decoration. It is the reader's road map.

## 8. Paragraph Repair

### Beat Captions

- 0:50: Replace repeated nouns when the meaning stays clear.
- 1:18: Use detail and punctuation to guide the reader.

### Voice Script

Now we use grammar like editors. Editing is not just hunting for mistakes. Editing means making the writing clearer for a reader. First, check every sentence. Does it have a subject? Does it have a predicate? If a sentence says, Ran through the park, it needs a subject. Who ran? Maya ran through the park. Next, look for repeated nouns. Maya opened Maya's notebook because Maya wanted Maya's story to be neat. We can repair it: Maya opened her notebook because she wanted her story to be neat. Then check details. Are adjectives and adverbs helping, or just crowding the sentence? The small red kite rose quickly gives a useful picture. Finally, check capitals, commas, and end marks. A paragraph does not need to be fancy to be strong. It needs to be clear, complete, and easy to follow. Here is the editor's order: sentence parts first, word choices second, punctuation third. When you edit in that order, you fix the meaning before you polish the surface.
